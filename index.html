Malama i Na Honu: Protecting Since 2007

Our mission is to “protect Hawaiian Green Sea Turtles through education, public awareness and conservation, all in the Spirit of Aloha.” In 2007, under the authority of the Endangered Species Act, NOAA began providing grant funds to the non-profit organization Malama I Na Honu to support public outreach activities that promote respectful behavior around Honu. Malama I Na Honus presence on the beach has significantly reduced disturbances to basking turtles ever since.

The Story of Hawaii's Turtles

Honu are as much a part of Malama i na Honu's ohana as they are the entire island chain's.

Cultural Significance

Indigenous to the Hawaiian Islands, Sea turtles hold immense significance to Hawaiian culture and traditions. They occupy a special place in the moolelo (stories) and the Kumulipo, the Hawaiian creation chant as the green turtle, known as "honu," and the hawksbill turtle, known as "honu-ea," are mentioned in the fourth verse of the Kumulipo, emphasizing their cultural importance. These majestic creatures are still revered by certain families as their spiritual guardians, referred to as aumākua.

Historical Role

Throughout history, sea turtles have been an integral part of native practices, religious ceremonies, and even the local diet. The shells, bones, and oil of sea turtles were utilized to create essential items such as fish hooks, tools, medicine, and jewelry. The traditional management practices, guided by the kapu system (a set of cultural rules and code of conduct) established by Chiefs or Ali‘i, played a crucial role in regulating the harvest of sea turtles. Today, harvesting, hunting, or otherwise harming any sea turtle in Hawaii is illegal.

Learn More

Population Decline

In the past, honu populations thrived and nested across the entirety of the Hawaiian Archipelago. However, with the arrival of European colonization, the kapu prohibition gradually eroded. By the 20th century, the commercialization of sea turtle harvesting led to a significant decline in their numbers, posing a serious threat to their survival. Luckily, due to work from organization like Malama Na Hong and legislative conservation actions, honu populations are recovering.

Current Policy & Regulations

In Hawaii, it is illegal to be nearer than 10ft to a turtle, both on land and in the water. Turtles often look like rocks under the waters surface or on the shore. Take extra care on your visit. For more more specifics, click below.

Who We Are

  • Our Volunteers

    Our organization is made up of over 95 dedicated and knowledgeable volunteer "Honu Guardians" who are on the beach every day of the year to prevent intentional and inadvertent harrassment of the Hawaiian Green Sea Trutles as they bask peacefully on the beach and to provide education outreach to the public by imparting facts about the turtles, explaining their behaviors, and creating an awareness of the ongoing need to protect this threatened species.
